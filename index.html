Problem Framing

Capacity issues were not caused by one factor alone.

The line needed a clearer way to see how cycle time variation, workstation load, and labor assignment affected daily output. Instead of relying only on averages, the model compared how the system behaved under different production conditions.

  • ✓Mapped the end-to-end production sequence and defined workstation-level inputs.
  • ✓Used time study data to estimate cycle times, utilization, and waiting points.
  • ✓Tested alternative staffing plans to reduce overload at high-demand steps.
  • ✓Compared baseline and improved scenarios using throughput and labor efficiency.

Decision output

Methodology

A structured improvement workflow from observation to scenario validation.

Map the Line

Defined each workstation, process sequence, queue point, labor role, and material handoff in the production flow.

Measure Cycle Times

Used time study observations to estimate processing time, variability, utilization, and idle time at key steps.

Build Simulation

Created a digital representation of the line to evaluate bottlenecks, workstation capacity, and labor allocation.

Compare Scenarios

Tested staffing and balancing alternatives to identify the best improvement path for higher output and efficiency.

Key Findings

What the analysis showed

1

Assembly was the main constraint.

The highest utilization appeared at the assembly station, which limited total line output during peak demand.

2

Small labor shifts had measurable impact.

Reallocating operators across high-load steps improved flow without requiring a complete process redesign.

3

Scenario testing reduced decision risk.

The model helped compare staffing and production plans before changing the real line.

4

Balanced capacity improved system output.

The recommended scenario projected a 14% throughput increase and 10% labor efficiency gain.
